# Packaging (Debian / Ubuntu)

A focused sub-course on the `.deb` packaging world — `dpkg`, `apt`, repositories, and how to build packages yourself. Split out from the main Linux lessons because it's a self-contained topic with its own toolchain, but it leans on the core lessons (disk, systemd) and they link back here.

Same house style as the main course: concept-first, analogy-heavy, *why* before *how*, with glossaries and pointers onward.

## Lessons

### 01 — [Package Management: `dpkg`, `apt`, `apt-get`, `aptitude`](01-package-management.md)

The lesson behind a real broken `full-upgrade`. Built on a **two-layer supply-chain model**: `dpkg` is the dock worker who installs *one* already-present `.deb` crate (no deps, no network) and keeps the ledger in `/var/lib/dpkg`; `apt`/`apt-get`/`aptitude` are the logistics manager that resolves the **dependency graph**, fetches from **repositories**, and hands crates to `dpkg` in order. Centers on the **two-phase install** (unpacked → configured) and the **maintainer scripts** (`preinst`/`postinst`/`prerm`/`postrm`) — and shows exactly how a bad `$TMPDIR` made the configure phase fail across many packages at once. Covers the `dpkg -l` **state codes** (`ii`/`iU`/`hi`), `apt` vs `apt-get` vs `aptitude`, **repositories** (sources.list(.d), codenames, components, deb822, `signed-by` keys, viewing/updating/upgrading the roster), `update` vs `upgrade` vs `full-upgrade`, **held** vs **kept-back** packages, **metapackages**, `autoremove`, and how to spot **upgradable/held/downgraded** packages (`apt-cache policy`, `/var/log/apt/history.log`). Ends with the **recovery playbook** (`dpkg --configure -a` → `apt-get -f install`) and the **safe-upgrade pattern** (force `TMPDIR=/tmp`, simulate with `-s`, then apply).

### 02 — [Building Your Own `.deb`](02-building-your-own-deb.md)

The hands-on payoff to Lesson 01: **you become the supplier** and hand-assemble a working package, so the two-phase install and the four maintainer scripts stop being abstract. Built on the insight that **a `.deb` is just a directory, compressed** — a build tree that mirrors the target filesystem, plus a `DEBIAN/` control folder. You build `ticktock`, a tiny systemd service package, writing the **`control`** file (fields, `Depends`, `Architecture`), the **`conffiles`** list (the real mechanism behind `remove` vs `purge`), and all four **maintainer scripts** by hand (with proper `$1`-argument branching and idempotency). Then `dpkg-deb --build`, inspect with `dpkg-deb --info/--contents` + `lintian`, install and **watch the "Unpacking → Setting up" phases live**. The centerpiece: **deliberately break `postinst` to reproduce the `iF` half-configured state** from the broken-upgrade incident, then recover it with `dpkg --configure -a` — feeling Lesson 01 §9 from the inside. Closes by exercising **upgrade** (conffile-preservation prompt) and **remove vs purge**, then maps the real **`debian/` + `debhelper`/`dh`/`dpkg-buildpackage`** source-package toolchain that automates everything you did by hand.

### 03 — [Snap & Flatpak: the Other Packaging Worlds](03-snap-and-flatpak.md)

The *other* two packaging worlds now coexisting with `apt` on Ubuntu. Built on the core shift from **shared shelves** (apt: one system-wide copy of each library) to **sealed containers** (each app brings its own dependencies), and the three pillars that follow — **self-contained, sandboxed, auto-updating** — each presented with its mirror-image cost (disk duplication, sandbox friction, loss of update control). Covers **Snap** as Canonical's vertically-integrated world (squashfs `loop` mounts — finally explaining the `/snap/...` clutter in `df -h`; the single proprietary store; channels stable/candidate/beta/edge; strict/classic/devmode confinement; plugs/slots interfaces; forced auto-refresh and the controversy) and **Flatpak** as the decentralized freedesktop answer (remotes vs one store, Flathub, shared **runtimes** + OSTree dedup, bubblewrap + XDG portals, per-app permissions/Flatseal, user vs system installs). The practical heart: **why the same program (Firefox!) is installed two or three ways at once** — the apt→snap transitional shim — and **how to tell which one you're actually running** (`readlink -f $(which app)`, then `dpkg -l`/`snap list`/`flatpak list`). Plus a quick word on **AppImage**, a Snap-vs-Flatpak table, a when-to-use-which guide, and the untangling cheatsheet.
